Question One part one

How would you describe yourself as a designer?

"I like to explore the relationships between nature, technology, and humanity."

Answer One part one

Neri Oxman, Leonardo Da Vinci, Olafur Eliasson, Claire Elise Boucher (Grimes), Margaret Atwood, and Ursula K Le Guin are the creatives by which I am most inspired. Based on the work I have produced this year and the way I intend to work going forward, I would describe myself as a transdisciplinary designer. I most enjoy working on projects which facilitate research and experimentation at the intersection of design, science, art, and engineering. Through my work I like to explore the relationships between nature, technology, and humanity because it occurs to me that despite our views of modern technology as being somewhat contrary to the natural world, human creations such as the internet are in fact as natural as nests or beehives. I therefore aim to question our definition of ‘natural,’ our fear, distrust, and demonisation of emergent technologies, and the relationships between both. I feel that I demonstrated this in in work which I completed for the Design Machines project this semester, for which I created a Processing program which receives information from an accompanying Arduino-powered ‘control’ panel, which the collaborator inputs via an array of potentiometers and switches. The program then draws lines across the radius of a circle (mimicking a radar display) which are controlled by this information, which allows the user to draw entirely unique flowers in real time. Radar displays are often # associated with military usage, but my machine draws flowers.

Recently, I read an online article by TJ Sidhu for The Face magazine about the writer’s experience of attending Metaverse Fashion Week, which was concluded by rather dramatically imploring the reader to “Choose life, choose a hobby, choose going out, choose friends, choose pubs … choose sleeping, choose waking up. Choose The Real World.” The entire article is so extremely techno pessimistic that the writer actually implies that attending a fashion show in the metaverse will automatically prevent you from going hiking or meeting friends in-person during the same lifetime. Ultimately, it is this concept of technology severing us from ‘real’ nature and thus destroying our ‘natural’ humanity that I wish to challenge in my work by utilising design to reveal the similarities between the mechanics of both nature and technology, and that our humanity is and always has been intrinsically embedded at their intersection.

Question Two

What makes a good piece of graphic design?

"I think a piece of graphic design can be considered ‘good’ when the graphic designer fully considers not only how best to accurately communicate their message and ideas within the piece of work that they are designing, but also how to communicate that message well to their audience."

Answer Two

My favourite example of outstanding design work and thinking is Aguahoja, an architectural structure made by 3D printing materials which are derived from fallen leaves, apple skins, and shrimp shells. It was created by the mediated matter group at MIT led by Professor Neri Oxman. The purpose of the project is to envision a future where abundant biocompatible materials sourced from healthy ecosystems replace current industrial materials used for architecture which are damaging to the environment. The structure is completely biocompatible and decays gradually when in contact with water – this decay was ‘programmed’ into the design to avoid obsolescence and waste, and to support the ecosystem it is placed in. This project inspired me to return to higher education to pursue a formal education in design, and it has largely formed my perception of what I consider to be a good piece of design. It embodies and accurately communicates the designers’ vision and message; the designers clearly considered every aspect of the final work and how it was achieved – extensive research into materials and methods of production which best aligned with the values and aims of the project was evidently undertaken. They adhered to a code of ethics regarding the collection of materials such as shrimp shells and leaves. The environmental impact at every stage of the piece’s life cycle was considered – how it was fabricated, its impact while it was on display, and what happened to it when no longer in use.

I think a piece of graphic design can be considered ‘good’ if it achieves the criteria for a good piece of design as described above and also when the graphic designer fully considers not only how best to accurately communicate their message and ideas within the piece of work that they are designing, but also how to communicate that message well to their audience. I think if the message is clear and simultaneously conveyed in a way that best addresses and resonates with the intended audience the design will be good, regardless of the aesthetic standard or level of technical expertise of the designer – I do, however, believe that technical expertise, experience in practice, and a strong knowledge of design theory can enhance the designer’s ability to achieve this and be more impactful where they do.

Question Three

What cultural artifact has influenced your thinking/mentality as a designer?

"Through sci-fi I gained awareness that the way things work is not a default and are rather a result of our decision making as a society and as individuals."

Answer Three

I think that design is largely about problem solving - figuring out how and why something works the way that it does and then figuring out how to make it work better or designing something new which works well when finished. This requires the designer to speculate about the future. This could mean considering how a poster could most effectively communicate its message and the best-case scenario if it does, or as with Aguahoja it could mean reimagining an entire industry or system such as architecture. This way of thinking speculatively about the future of society and human relationships with technology was first introduced to me through the sci-fi genre. Through sci-fi I gained awareness that the way things work is not a default and are rather a result of our decision making as a society and as individuals. This inspired better critical thinking about how the world is, because I can first ‘play’ with and speculate about how it could be without the assumptions and biases I was otherwise unaware I had, whether I think that hypothetical way could be better, and for whom it might be better for. I think this also facilitates better critique and self-reflection regarding my own work and practice as I can think about a finished work as a synecdoche and consider what kind of a society it could be synecdochic of and how that matches up against the society I want to be part of building. This was important in the Discover Dundonian service designed by my group and I for the groupwork part of this semester’s agency project. A crucial part of defining the outcome was our consideration of the impact that the service would have on the community we were designing it for, whether that impact was positive, negative, or merely non-existent, and how to ensure that its functionality was optimised for the people we imagined would interact with it.

Question Four part one

Do graphic designers have an ethical responsibility?

"Visual communication is an equally important and prevalent form of communication as verbal or written communication."

Answer Four part one

Communication is what creates and shapes our culture, and our culture defines what we as a society and as individuals deem to be morally acceptable or unacceptable. It is a common misconception that the primary concern of graphic design is aesthetics or decoration – I was once asked by a co-worker if I could make something “look more Apple-y”, but graphic design is more focused on producing effective visual communication, than simply making things look more pleasing. Now more than ever before, visual communication is an equally important and prevalent form of communication as verbal or written communication. Whether we are sharing information and ideas via advertisements, photographs, logos, icons, wayfinding symbols, packaging, games, memes, animations, etc., we are likely engaging with something which has been produced at least partially by a graphic designer. Therefore, I think that graphic designers absolutely should take responsibility for the messages and ideas they are releasing or aiding others to release into culture. For example, the term ‘greenwashing’ refers to when something is marketed as being environmentally friendly in order to appeal to environmentally conscious consumers, when it is in fact still harmful to the environment. Bamboo toothbrushes are notorious for being a product frequently plagued by greenwashing; the greenwashed bamboo toothbrush itself is more environmentally friendly than traditional plastic ones but is still packaged in plastic where an environmentally friendly alternative, such as card, is available and more appropriate. The finished greenwashed product is ‘better’ for the environment than the fully plastic alternative, but not actually environmentally friendly as it is described. If the graphic designer was not responsible for this decision, they would have at least been aware that the packaging they were designing ‘eco-friendly’ graphics for was made from plastic. This is not only environmentally irresponsible, but misleading to the consumer and contributes to culture that behaving in such a manner is morally acceptable, which perpetuates the act.

Question Four part two

What is yours?

"I wish to encourage humans to re-build connections with nature lost to pervasive anthropocentric thinking."

Answer Four part two

I aim to never knowingly nor intentionally produce work which miscommunicates about its subject, nor is purposefully misleading, and to take responsibility for ensuring that I am not ignorant about anything that is communicated by my work, particularly if there is potential for that message to be harmful. I also intend to only produce work which is not in conflict with my personal values – if I don’t believe something is ‘right’, I feel strongly that I shouldn’t produce work which promotes it to others. As discussed in my answer to previous questions, I would like to use my work to facilitate and encourage healthier relationships between humans and technology, but not in a way that is blindly optimistic and uncritical of the risk involved in the adoption or misuse of new technologies. I feel that discussing, addressing, and de-mystifying those are a crucial part of understanding and strengthening these relationships. I wish to encourage humans to re-build connections with nature lost to pervasive anthropocentric thinking – the thinking that humans have become so intelligent that we have superseded nature as planetary decision maker and become superior to everything else in the natural world; that what we create is somehow inherently isolated from or to the contrary and detriment of nature because we are so intellectually superior to nature that it ‘cannot comprehend’ what we have made. I aim to do so by properly respecting all forms of life – human or otherwise - as different but equal, and therefore rejecting to work with the paradigmatic assumption that human life, and certain human lives, are superior to all others. I believe that working without the assumption that ‘humans always know better’ will facilitate better quality thinking regarding the implementation of natural materials and systems within my work and encourage me to consider the implications of projects to everyone – not just humans or a limited demographic of humans.

Question Five part one

What are the strongest parts of your graphic design work to date?

"My interest in web design has enhanced my understanding of typography, grids, and layout across the board."

Answer Five part one

I feel that my strongest work has been work that does not necessarily fall under the category of graphic design and most of this work has been approached more as a learning opportunity or experiment, with the humble goal of expanding my skillset into areas that interest me rather than pressuring myself to produce a fantastic piece of graphic design. Particularly this year, I have focused on using each brief to encourage myself to become stronger at coding in preparation for the way I hope to work throughout fourth year – specifically; creative coding (such as generative art), electronics, and web development. I feel I have achieved this, and that as anticipated, my interest in web design has enhanced my understanding of typography, grids, and layout across the board. That said, I think that I still have a fair bit more to learn before I can consider the websites or programs that I am building to be strong examples. At this early stage in my learning process, the aspect I find to be strongest of any project I have undertaken involving code (which is both of them this semester) is simply that it all works, especially the generative art control panel and accompanying program I developed during Design Machines. Therefore, I think that the strongest aspects of my design work to date have been that in completing them, I have managed to learn the skills I wanted to learn whilst simultaneously producing what I think are interesting pieces of design which work as intended, answer the given brief, and prepare me for the work I hope to be engaged in next year, and ultimately beyond graduation.

Question Five part two

What are the weakest?

"I completely abandoned any attempt at a healthy work-life balance."

Answer Five part two

The weakest part of my design work to date has been my time management. I consistently either underestimate how much time it will take me to complete something, or I overestimate how much time I have to work through a project and set myself a far larger task than is realistically achievable in the timescale determined by the brief. This generally leaves me rushing and corner-cutting near the end to meet the deadline, which results in the work submitted being completed to a lower standard than I know myself to be capable of. However, this semester this was (*just about*) not the case. I would like to say that this is because I have learned my lesson, set myself realistic workloads and managed my time well, but in reality, I just completely abandoned any attempt at a healthy work-life balance and grossly overestimated how long it would take me to learn how to program the microcontroller for Design Machines, which gave me a little ‘extra’ time to work on other areas of the project. I think going forward I can set myself more realistic tasks to complete relative to the timeframe stipulated by the brief, which should ensure that I have time to finish everything to a standard I am satisfied with, and I can be stricter with myself about building down-time into my schedule so as to avoid becoming burnt-out and overwhelmed as deadlines draw near.
